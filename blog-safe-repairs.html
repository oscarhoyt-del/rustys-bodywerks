What the Research Says

A landmark study of collision-repaired vehicles found that approximately 90% of cars that had been through a body shop still had unresolved damage when independently inspected afterward. Not cosmetic issues. Structural problems. Safety system failures. Missed damage that the original shop simply didn't address.

Think about that for a moment. Nine out of ten repaired vehicles still have damage in them. The car looks fine on the outside. The paint is fresh. The panels are straight. But underneath — in the frame, the safety systems, the structural components — something was missed or cut short.

Why This Is a Life Safety Issue

Modern vehicles are engineered as complete safety systems. Every component — the frame, the crumple zones, the airbag sensors, the ADAS cameras, the structural welds — works together to protect occupants in a crash. When any part of that system is compromised by a poor repair, the entire safety envelope of the vehicle is degraded. Your car may look repaired. It may drive fine. But in the next accident, it may not protect you and your family the way it was designed to.

Who Is Responsible?

In the majority of cases we've seen, the vehicles came from insurance company preferred shops — what the industry calls Direct Repair Program or DRP shops. These are shops that have made a financial agreement with insurance companies to keep repair costs down in exchange for a steady stream of referrals.

"The shop took shortcuts to keep the insurance company happy and neglected the most important party — the vehicle owner and their family."

— Rusty, Owner, Rusty's Bodywerks

We want to be clear: we are not saying every DRP shop does poor work. But the financial incentive structure of those arrangements creates pressure to cut corners. And when corners are cut on a collision repair, the person who pays the price isn't the insurance company. It's the family driving the vehicle.

Required Post-Repair Safety Inspections — and Why Most Shops Skip Them

Most vehicle manufacturers now require specific safety inspections after a collision repair. These aren't optional add-ons. They are part of the manufacturer's repair procedure — the same procedures that determine whether your vehicle performs as designed in a crash.

These required inspections include:

  • Restraint system scanning — verifying airbag modules, seatbelt pretensioners, and crash sensors were properly reset
  • Structural measurement verification — confirming the frame is within manufacturer tolerances
  • ADAS recalibration — resetting cameras, radar, and sensors that affect automatic safety systems
  • Corrosion protection inspection — verifying anti-corrosion materials were applied to repaired areas
  • Squeeze-type resistance spot weld verification — confirming structural welds meet factory specifications

Most shops skip these inspections entirely. They add time and cost to the repair. Insurance companies don't like paying for them. So they don't happen.

What You Can Do to Protect Your Family

If your vehicle has been in an accident — even a minor one — here is what we strongly recommend:

  1. Choose your own repair shop. In Texas, you have the legal right to choose any licensed body shop. Do not let your insurance company make this decision for you.
  2. Ask specifically about post-repair safety inspections. Any reputable shop should be able to tell you exactly which manufacturer-required inspections they perform and document them in writing.
  3. Ask if they follow OEM repair procedures. If the answer is vague or uncertain, walk away.
  4. If your car was already repaired elsewhere and you have any doubts — get a post-repair inspection. It could be the most important thing you do for your family's safety.
